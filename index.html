Research Vision

My work sits at the intersection of classical pavement mechanics and data-driven infrastructure intelligence. I aim to enhance the resilience of road infrastructure against extreme climate conditions — particularly icing events and thermal stress — by coupling physics-based failure models with physics-constrained machine learning.

Where traditional approaches rely on empirical design charts, I develop methods that are simultaneously interpretable to engineers and scalable at the network level. The goal: infrastructure that doesn't just survive today's loads, but adaptable to tomorrow's climate extremes.

Overview

Binary classification of icing accident risk on Korean national highways. Integrated weather stations, road geometry, and historical crash records into a unified feature matrix.

Methodology

Random Forest with stratified k-fold cross-validation. Feature engineering from meteorological variables (temperature drop rate, dew point delta) and road geometry attributes. Addressed class imbalance via SMOTE.

Physics-Informed ML for Pavement Distress Prediction

In Progress — M.S. Research

Overview

Developing a PIML framework that embeds pavement constitutive equations (fatigue, thermal cracking) as hard constraints within neural network training — combining physical interpretability with data efficiency.

Methodology

Physics loss terms derived from fracture mechanics (Paris' Law, Eshelby inclusion theory) added to standard MSE loss. Architecture: residual network with physics-constrained output layers trained on pavement monitoring sensor data.

PyTorchPIMLParis' Law

Impact

Targeting improved generalization with limited labeled field data — a critical bottleneck in pavement condition monitoring. Expected to demonstrate physics-consistent predictions even in data-sparse pavement sections.
